# Investing Fundamentals

## Why Invest?

Inflation erodes the purchasing power of cash over time. At 6% annual inflation, R100 today will only buy R74 worth of goods in 5 years. Investing allows your money to grow faster than inflation, building real wealth over time.

## Asset Classes

### Equities (Stocks)
Ownership shares in companies. Returns come from price appreciation and dividends.
- Historical long-run return: ~7–10% per year (real, after inflation)
- High volatility — expect 20–40% drawdowns in bear markets
- Best suited for long time horizons (5+ years)

### Bonds (Fixed Income)
Loans to governments or corporations in exchange for regular interest payments.
- Lower returns than equities (typically 2–5%)
- Lower volatility — provide stability in a portfolio
- Inverse relationship with interest rates: when rates rise, bond prices fall

### Real Estate
Property held directly or via REITs (Real Estate Investment Trusts).
- Income from rent; appreciation from property value growth
- REITs provide real estate exposure without direct ownership
- Less liquid than stocks and bonds

### Cash and Cash Equivalents
Savings accounts, money market funds, Treasury bills.
- Lowest return but highest liquidity and stability
- Essential for emergency funds and short-term goals

### Commodities
Physical goods: gold, silver, oil, agricultural products.
- Often used as inflation hedge (especially gold)
- High volatility and no income generation
- Typically a small allocation (5–10%) in diversified portfolios

### Cryptocurrency
Digital assets: Bitcoin, Ethereum, etc.
- Extremely high volatility — can lose 50–80% of value in bear markets
- No underlying cash flows to anchor valuation
- Keep allocation small (0–5%) if included at all

## Diversification

Diversification is the practice of spreading investments across multiple asset classes, geographies, and sectors to reduce risk without proportionally reducing expected return.

**Why it works:** Different assets respond differently to economic conditions. When equities fall, bonds often rise. When domestic markets struggle, international markets may outperform.

**Diversification does not:**
- Eliminate all risk (systematic/market risk remains)
- Guarantee returns
- Protect against a global financial crisis

## Risk Profiles

### Conservative
- Priority: capital preservation
- Typical allocation: 60% bonds, 30% equities, 10% cash
- Suited for: retirees, short time horizons, low risk tolerance

### Moderate
- Priority: balance between growth and stability
- Typical allocation: 50% equities, 40% bonds, 10% other
- Suited for: medium time horizons (5–15 years), average risk tolerance

### Aggressive
- Priority: maximum long-term growth
- Typical allocation: 75% equities, 15% bonds, 10% other
- Suited for: long time horizons (15+ years), high risk tolerance, strong income

## Key Investment Metrics

### Sharpe Ratio
Measures return earned per unit of risk taken.
- Formula: (Portfolio Return − Risk-Free Rate) / Portfolio Volatility
- Above 1.0: good risk-adjusted return
- Above 2.0: excellent
- Below 0.5: poor — consider rebalancing

### Asset Allocation Drift
Over time, strong-performing assets grow to represent a larger share of the portfolio than intended. Rebalancing — selling some of the outperformer and buying underperformers — restores target allocations and enforces buy-low-sell-high discipline.

Rebalance when any asset class drifts more than 5% from its target.

## Compound Growth

The most powerful force in investing is compounding — earning returns on prior returns.

Example: R10,000 invested at 8% annually:
- After 10 years: R21,589
- After 20 years: R46,610
- After 30 years: R100,627

Starting early matters more than starting with a large amount. An extra 10 years of compounding doubles the outcome.

## Index Funds and ETFs

Low-cost index funds and exchange-traded funds (ETFs) that track broad market indices (e.g. S&P 500, JSE All Share) are the recommended starting point for most investors because:
- Lower fees than actively managed funds
- Broad diversification in a single purchase
- Historically outperform most active fund managers over 10+ years
- Simple to understand and monitor

## Common Investing Mistakes

1. **Timing the market** — missing the 10 best days in a decade significantly reduces returns. Time in the market beats timing the market.
2. **Panic selling** — selling during downturns locks in losses and misses the recovery.
3. **Chasing past performance** — last year's top fund is rarely next year's.
4. **Ignoring fees** — a 1% annual fee difference compounds to a massive difference over 30 years.
5. **Concentration risk** — holding too much in a single stock, sector, or country.
6. **Neglecting tax efficiency** — using tax-advantaged accounts (TFSA, RA in South Africa) where available.
