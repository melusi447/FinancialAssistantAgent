# Debt Management

## Types of Debt

**Good debt** — borrowing that builds wealth or generates income over time:
- Mortgage on a primary residence (builds equity, potential appreciation)
- Student loans for high-return qualifications
- Business loans with positive return on investment

**Bad debt** — borrowing for depreciating assets or consumption:
- Credit card balances carried month-to-month (typically 15–25% interest)
- Personal loans for lifestyle purchases
- Buy-now-pay-later schemes used irresponsibly
- Car loans on vehicles you cannot afford

## Debt Repayment Strategies

### Avalanche Method (Mathematically Optimal)
Pay minimum payments on all debts, then direct any extra money to the debt with the **highest interest rate** first.

- Minimises total interest paid over time
- Typically saves hundreds to thousands compared to the Snowball method
- Best for people who are motivated by numbers and long-term savings
- May take longer to eliminate the first debt if it has a large balance

### Snowball Method (Psychologically Powerful)
Pay minimum payments on all debts, then direct any extra money to the debt with the **smallest balance** first.

- Provides quick wins that maintain motivation
- Slightly more interest paid overall compared to Avalanche
- Best for people who have struggled to stick to repayment plans before
- Each eliminated debt frees up cash flow for the next

### Which to Choose
Research suggests the Snowball method leads to better completion rates for people with multiple debts, even though Avalanche saves more money mathematically. Choose the method you will actually stick to.

## Interest Rate Benchmarks

| Rate | Strategy |
|---|---|
| Below 5% | Consider investing instead of aggressively paying down |
| 5–10% | Balanced approach — split extra cash between debt and investing |
| Above 10% | Prioritise debt repayment before investing |
| Above 15% | Aggressively eliminate before any other financial goal |
| Above 20% | Emergency — stop all non-essential spending and eliminate immediately |

## Credit Cards

Credit cards are tools, not income. Used responsibly:
- Pay the **full statement balance** every month — not just the minimum
- Never carry a balance above 30% of your credit limit (affects credit score)
- Use cards with rewards only if you pay in full monthly
- Never use a credit card for cash advances (fees + immediate interest)

If you carry a balance, the effective cost of every purchase is the purchase price plus interest until the balance is cleared.

## Debt Consolidation

Consolidating multiple debts into a single lower-interest loan can reduce total interest and simplify repayment. It makes sense when:
- The consolidation rate is meaningfully lower than your average current rate
- You address the spending behaviour that created the debt
- You do not extend the repayment term so long that you pay more total interest

Debt consolidation does not reduce the principal owed — it only changes the interest rate and structure.

## Minimum Payments Warning

Paying only the minimum on a credit card can extend repayment to 10–25 years on a balance that could be cleared in 2–3 years with disciplined extra payments. Always pay more than the minimum if possible.
