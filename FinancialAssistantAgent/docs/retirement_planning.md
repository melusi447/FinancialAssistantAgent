# Retirement Planning

## How Much Do You Need?

The most widely used rule of thumb is the **4% Rule** (also called the Safe Withdrawal Rate):

- At retirement, you can withdraw 4% of your portfolio in year one, then adjust for inflation each year, with a high probability of the portfolio lasting 30 years.
- To retire on a specific annual income, divide that amount by 0.04 (or multiply by 25).

**Example:**
- Desired retirement income: R600,000 per year
- Required retirement portfolio: R600,000 ÷ 0.04 = **R15,000,000**

A more conservative version uses 3% withdrawal rate (multiply desired income by ~33).

## The Power of Starting Early

The single most important variable in retirement planning is time. Due to compound growth:

| Monthly Contribution | Start Age | Retirement Age | Portfolio (at 8% return) |
|---|---|---|---|
| R3,000 | 25 | 65 | ~R10,500,000 |
| R3,000 | 35 | 65 | ~R4,500,000 |
| R3,000 | 45 | 65 | ~R1,750,000 |

Starting 10 years earlier more than doubles the outcome with the same monthly contribution.

## South African Retirement Vehicles

### Retirement Annuity (RA)
- Contributions are tax-deductible up to 27.5% of taxable income (max R350,000 per year)
- Growth is tax-free inside the fund
- At retirement: up to one-third as a lump sum (first R550,000 tax-free), remainder as annuity income
- Cannot access funds before age 55 (with limited exceptions)
- Recommended for: self-employed individuals and those without employer pension funds

### Pension Fund (Employer)
- Employer and employee contribute
- Employee contributions are tax-deductible
- Similar tax treatment to RAs at retirement
- On resignation: can transfer to preservation fund, new employer fund, or RA — avoid cashing out

### Provident Fund
- Similar to pension fund
- Historically the full amount could be taken as a lump sum at retirement (transitioning to align with pension fund rules)

### Tax-Free Savings Account (TFSA)
- Annual contribution limit: R36,000
- Lifetime contribution limit: R500,000
- All growth (interest, dividends, capital gains) is completely tax-free
- No tax on withdrawal
- Most flexible tax-advantaged vehicle — no lock-in
- Best used for: medium to long-term goals, including retirement as a supplement to RA

### Living Annuity vs Guaranteed Annuity
At retirement, you must purchase an annuity with at least two-thirds of your retirement fund:
- **Living annuity:** you remain invested and draw down 2.5–17.5% per year. Investment risk remains with you. Remaining balance passes to heirs.
- **Guaranteed annuity:** insurer pays a fixed income for life. No investment risk. No residual estate value.
- Many retirees use a combination of both.

## Contribution Benchmarks

A common guideline is to save 15% of gross income for retirement throughout your career. If you start late, you need to save more:

| Start Age | Required Savings Rate (for comfortable retirement at 65) |
|---|---|
| 25 | ~10–12% |
| 30 | ~15% |
| 35 | ~20% |
| 40 | ~25–30% |
| 45+ | 35%+ (or adjust retirement age) |

## Sequence of Returns Risk

The order of investment returns matters enormously in the years just before and after retirement. A major market crash in the first 5 years of retirement can permanently impair a portfolio even if long-run average returns are the same.

Mitigation strategies:
- Hold 2–3 years of expenses in cash/bonds at retirement
- Reduce equity allocation in the 5 years before retirement
- Use a flexible withdrawal strategy (reduce withdrawals in down years if possible)

## Common Retirement Planning Mistakes

1. **Starting too late** — every decade of delay roughly halves the outcome
2. **Cashing out pension on resignation** — destroys decades of compounding and triggers tax
3. **Underestimating longevity** — plan for 30+ years in retirement
4. **Ignoring inflation** — retirement income needs to grow with inflation
5. **Over-conservative portfolio in retirement** — with a 30-year horizon, some equity exposure is still needed
6. **Not maximising tax deductions** — RA contributions up to 27.5% of income are deductible
