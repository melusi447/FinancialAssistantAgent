# Personal Finance Basics

## The 50/30/20 Rule

The 50/30/20 rule is a simple budgeting framework that divides after-tax income into three categories:

- **50% Needs** — essential expenses you cannot avoid: rent/mortgage, utilities, groceries, minimum debt payments, insurance, transport to work
- **30% Wants** — lifestyle choices: dining out, entertainment, subscriptions, holidays, gym memberships
- **20% Savings and debt repayment** — emergency fund, retirement contributions, extra debt payments, investments

If your needs exceed 50%, look for ways to reduce fixed costs such as refinancing, moving, or switching providers. If savings fall below 20%, reduce wants before touching needs.

## Emergency Fund

An emergency fund is cash set aside exclusively for unexpected expenses or income loss.

**Target:** 3–6 months of essential living expenses in a liquid, accessible account (e.g. a high-yield savings account).

- Start with a starter emergency fund of 1 month's expenses
- Build to 3 months before aggressively paying down low-interest debt
- Extend to 6 months if self-employed, in a volatile industry, or a single-income household
- Keep it separate from your everyday account to reduce temptation
- Do not invest your emergency fund — capital preservation and liquidity matter more than returns

## Net Worth

Net worth = Total assets − Total liabilities

- **Assets:** savings, investments, property value, vehicles, valuables
- **Liabilities:** mortgage balance, car loans, credit card balances, student loans, personal loans

Track net worth monthly or quarterly. A growing net worth over time — even slowly — indicates financial progress. Short-term fluctuations in investment values are normal and expected.

## Cash Flow

Monthly cash flow = Income − Expenses

Positive cash flow means you are living within your means. Negative cash flow means you are spending more than you earn and accumulating debt. Improving cash flow requires either increasing income, reducing expenses, or both.

## Financial Goals Framework

Use the SMART framework for financial goals:
- **Specific** — "Save R50,000 for a house deposit" not "save money"
- **Measurable** — track progress monthly
- **Achievable** — realistic given your current income and expenses
- **Relevant** — aligned with your values and life plans
- **Time-bound** — "by December 2026"

Prioritise goals in this order:
1. Emergency fund (1 month)
2. Employer retirement match (free money)
3. High-interest debt repayment (above 10%)
4. Emergency fund (3–6 months)
5. Retirement contributions
6. Other goals (house deposit, education, travel)
