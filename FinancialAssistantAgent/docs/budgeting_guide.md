# Budgeting Guide

## Why Budgeting Works

A budget is not a restriction — it is a plan for where your money goes before you spend it. People who budget consistently accumulate more wealth at every income level than those who do not, because they make intentional spending decisions rather than reactive ones.

## Common Budgeting Methods

### Zero-Based Budgeting
Every rand of income is assigned a purpose so that income minus all allocations equals zero.
- Most detailed and intentional method
- Best for people who want full control and have variable expenses
- Requires monthly setup

### Envelope Method
Physical or digital "envelopes" for each spending category. When the envelope is empty, spending stops.
- Highly effective for controlling discretionary spending
- Works well with cash or budgeting apps
- Prevents category overspending automatically

### 50/30/20 Rule
The simplest approach: 50% needs, 30% wants, 20% savings/debt.
- Easy to implement and maintain
- Less granular than zero-based but adequate for most people
- Best starting point for those new to budgeting

### Pay Yourself First
Automatically transfer savings and investments on payday before spending anything.
- Removes willpower from the equation
- Ensures savings goals are met before lifestyle expenses
- Can be combined with any other method

## Spending Categories and Benchmarks

| Category | Recommended % of Net Income |
|---|---|
| Housing (rent/mortgage) | 25–30% |
| Food (groceries + eating out) | 10–15% |
| Transport (car, fuel, public transport) | 10–15% |
| Utilities (electricity, water, internet) | 5–10% |
| Insurance | 5–10% |
| Entertainment | 5–10% |
| Personal care | 2–5% |
| Clothing | 2–5% |
| Savings / investments | 15–20% |
| Debt repayment (above minimums) | As much as possible |

## Tracking Expenses

The three most important categories to track closely are typically:
1. **Food** — restaurant and takeaway spending is frequently underestimated
2. **Entertainment/subscriptions** — small recurring costs accumulate significantly
3. **Impulse purchases** — unplanned shopping trips and online purchases

Methods for tracking:
- Budgeting apps (22Seven in South Africa, YNAB, Mint)
- Bank transaction exports to a spreadsheet
- Manual daily recording in a notebook or notes app

## Identifying Leaks

Common budget leaks that are frequently overlooked:
- Unused subscriptions (streaming services, gym memberships, apps)
- Bank fees — negotiate or switch to lower-fee accounts
- Convenience spending (daily coffee, frequent takeaways)
- Emotional or stress spending
- Bank charges on insufficient funds or overdrafts

Review bank statements for the past 3 months to identify patterns before building a budget.

## Irregular Expenses

Many people budget only for monthly bills and are caught off guard by annual or irregular costs. Divide all annual costs by 12 and include them in your monthly budget as a sinking fund:
- Car licence and insurance renewal
- Annual subscriptions
- School fees
- Holiday spending
- Medical costs not covered by medical aid

## Adjusting Your Budget

A budget is not set once and forgotten. Review it:
- Monthly — compare actuals vs plan
- When income changes (salary increase, job change)
- When major life events occur (marriage, child, move)
- Quarterly — reassess whether categories reflect your actual priorities

A budget that does not reflect your real life will not be followed. It is better to have a realistic budget that is slightly generous on discretionary spending than an aspirational budget that is abandoned after two weeks.

## Budgeting with an Irregular Income

For freelancers, commission earners, or those with variable income:
1. Calculate your minimum reliable monthly income (use the lowest of the past 12 months)
2. Build your budget around that floor
3. In high-income months, direct the surplus to an income-smoothing account
4. Draw from that account in low-income months to maintain consistent budget allocations
5. Maintain a larger emergency fund (6–12 months) to buffer income gaps
